The Neurodevelopmental Disorders Ecological Tracker (N-DET) Pilot Validation Study

Welcome and Thank You!

Hello,

Thank you for participating in this pilot study and for taking the time to read this short introduction. We’d like to briefly explain the purpose of the study and provide a few important instructions before you begin.

This study is part of our initial validation of the Neurodevelopmental Disorders Ecological Tracker (N-DET) — a new tool designed to assess daily behaviors and symptoms commonly associated with neurodevelopmental conditions. There is a real need for tools that can capture these behaviors in everyday life, especially to track change over time or in response to interventions. We hope the N-DET will help fill this gap.

You are among the first parents to try out this tool. Each evening, the m-Path app will prompt you to complete a brief survey about your child’s behavior during that day. For each item, you will have the option to indicate whether the item was not relevant for your child or if you didn’t have the opportunity to observe the behavior. You’ll also be able to add open-text comments—we warmly welcome your feedback.

What Will You Be Doing?

Once a day, in the evening, you’ll receive a reminder from the m-Path app to complete a short survey based on your observations of your child throughout the day. Although the same questions will appear each day, this repetition is important for understanding daily patterns and variability.

Three Things to Keep in Mind:

  1. Answer Accurately and Spontaneously: Please answer each item based on your child’s behavior during that specific day. Respond as accurately as possible, but without overthinking. This helps keep the process smooth and consistent.
  2. Understand the Questions as Intended: We know that different people may interpret items differently. That’s why for each item, we’ve included a short explanation and examples to clarify what we mean. In the app, you can tap the “e.g.” link next to any item to view these clarifications. (Note: In this document, clarifications are provided directly below each item).
  3. Be Consistent Over Time: It’s important to keep your interpretation of the items consistent across the week. You can revisit this guide anytime, either here or through the m-Path app.

The items, along with their explanations and examples, appear below. Please read through them carefully before beginning the daily surveys.

Understanding the Survey Response Formats

In the survey, there will be a few response formats that are associated with specific sets of items. For many of the items the response scales will include:

Frequency: How often did this behavior occur today?

[We opted not to use specific numbers since many of the relevant behaviors can be continuous and that won’t work well with numbers]. By ‘today’ we mean during the time you spent today with your child.

  • Not at all: The behavior did not occur at any point during the day.
  • Rarely: Brief or isolated instances that were not a significant part of the day's activities. Would stand out as unusual occurrences when reflecting on the day.
  • Sometimes: The behavior occurred periodically throughout the day. Notable but not a dominant pattern of behavior.
  • Often: The behavior occurred regularly throughout the day. Multiple instances (6+ times) that formed a noticeable pattern. A significant feature of the child's behavior that day, but with clear gaps between occurrences.
  • Most of the day: The behavior occurred consistently throughout most of the day. The behavior was a persistent feature of the child's actions. Very few or no extended periods without the behavior being present.

Impact: How much did this behavior interfere with [Name]'s functioning today?

How much the behavior disrupted the child's ability to:

  • Complete daily activities and routines
  • Interact with family members and peers
  • Participate in learning or play
  • Handle typical daily demands
  • Navigate through their regular schedule

Note that this item measures the intensity of disruption each time the behavior occurred, regardless of frequency. A rare behavior can have severe impact, while a frequent behavior might have minimal impact.

  • Not at all: When the behavior occurred, it didn't disrupt activities or functioning. The behavior was noticeable but didn't require any changes or accommodations. The child could continue their normal activities without interruption.
  • Slightly: The behavior caused minimal disruption when it occurred. Brief, minor adjustments were needed but activities could continue. The child quickly returned to normal functioning with minimal support.
  • Moderately: When the behavior occurred, it noticeably disrupted current activities. Required some intervention or modification to continue activities. The child needed active support to maintain functioning.
  • Considerably: Each instance significantly disrupted whatever was happening. Required substantial intervention to resume activities. The child had difficulty returning to normal functioning. Activities often needed to be modified or paused.
  • Severely: When the behavior occurred, it completely disrupted current activities. Required immediate and intensive intervention. The child couldn't continue normal functioning until the situation was resolved. Activities had to be stopped or completely changed.

For example:

  • A tantrum that happens once but requires ending a playdate would have high impact.
  • Frequent humming that others can easily tune out would have low impact.
  • A single aggressive incident requiring medical attention would have severe impact.
  • Regular fidgeting that doesn't disturb others would have minimal impact.

Survey Items, Explanations, and Examples

The items involving these response scales include:

Aggression and Irritability

1. [Name] was in a negative or irritable mood.

Explanation: Showing signs of being unhappy, grumpy, easily frustrated, or quick to become upset.

Examples: Whining, complaining, being sensitive to minor issues, having difficulty being cheered up.

2. [Name] was uncooperative or defiant.

Explanation: Actively refusing to follow instructions, deliberately doing the opposite of what is asked, or showing resistance to requests.

Examples: Not following basic instructions within daily routine, refusing to participate in required activities at home or school, responding "no" to most requests.

3. [Name] displayed challenging behaviors.

Explanation: Exhibiting behaviors that are difficult to manage, disruptive, or concerning beyond typical age-appropriate behavior.

Examples: Physical aggression (hitting, kicking, biting), throwing objects in anger, intense tantrums, destructive behavior towards objects, running away/bolting.

Attention

4. [Name] had difficulty staying focused on a game/task for 3-4 minutes.

Explanation: Maintaining attention and engagement with one activity without switching to something else.

Examples: Completing a puzzle, staying with one game/toy, sitting for a meal without getting up frequently.

5. [Name] had difficulty concentrating for 3-4 minutes in the presence of distractions.

Explanation: Continuing to focus on an activity despite environmental distractions or competing stimuli.

Examples: Playing with a favorite toy while others move around, staying with an activity during noisy times, engaging with a preferred item despite nearby activities.

6. [Name] had difficulty paying attention to verbal, visual, or written information.

Explanation: Showing reduced ability to process and follow information presented in different forms.

Examples: Not noticing obstacles while walking/running, overlooking familiar objects/people nearby, missing when someone shows them a toy.

Hyperactivity

7. [Name] was impulsive and/or acted without thinking.

Explanation: Responding or acting quickly without considering consequences or safety.

Examples: Grabbing items from others, rushing into activities without waiting for instructions, touching items immediately despite warnings, running into spaces without checking.

8. [Name] had difficulty waiting.

Explanation: Showing signs of distress or inability to delay immediate wants or needs.

Examples: Unable to wait for their turn with toys, becoming upset when food isn't immediate, struggling when needs to wait in line, having trouble waiting for preferred activities, interrupting others or having trouble waiting their turn in a conversation.

9. [Name] was overly active and restless.

Explanation: Showing excessive movement or activity beyond what is appropriate for the situation.

Examples: Constant moving/fidgeting during seated activities, difficulty staying in one place, climbing on furniture when should be sitting, running instead of walking indoors.

Avoidance, Fearfulness, and Nervousness

For the following items, expression of fear, anxiety/distress, worry, or apprehension may manifest through statements, vocalizations, or physical signs such as trembling, shaking, sweating, difficulty breathing, physical tension, restlessness, pacing, or being easily startled.

10. [Name] showed fear of, or anxiety/distress about, or avoided, or withdrew from specific objects or situations.

Explanation: Displaying noticeable fear, distress, or avoidance behaviors when encountering particular objects, sounds, sensations, or situations that are typically not frightening to others.

Examples: Running away from or refusing to go near specific objects (e.g., vacuum cleaners, balloons, dogs), refusing to enter rooms where specific objects are present.

11. [Name] showed fear of, or anxiety/distress about social situations.

Explanation: Displaying significant discomfort, distress when faced with social interactions or situations involving other people.

Examples: Clutching or staying very close to caregiver in social settings, moving away when others come close, covering face with hands or objects.

12. [Name] showed fear of, or anxiety/distress about change, loss of routine, or disruption of desired activity.

Explanation: Exhibiting significant distress or negative reactions when faced with changes to normal routines, transitions, or interruptions to preferred activities.

Examples: Becoming upset when a familiar route is changed, becoming upset when having to wear different clothes than usual (e.g., when preferred outfit is in wash), becoming agitated when sitting in a different spot at the dinner table or in class.

13. [Name] showed fear of, or anxiety/distress about being separated from a caregiver and/or showed reluctance to be alone.

Explanation: Demonstrating significant distress when separated or potentially separated from primary caregivers, or showing excessive need for caregiver presence.

Examples: Becoming extremely upset at drop-off times, following caregiver around the house, refusing to sleep alone, frequent checking for caregiver's presence.

14. [Name] showed fear of, or anxiety/distress about, or avoided, or withdrew from any kind of situation.

Explanation: Displaying generalized anxiety, fear, or avoidance behaviors across various situations, beyond specific triggers or contexts.

Examples: Showing excessive worry about multiple everyday situations, frequently seeking reassurance about normal activities, avoiding new experiences in general, withdrawing from various age-appropriate activities.

RRBs (Repetitive Behaviors)

15. [Name] engaged in repetitive motor behaviors without functional motor purpose.

Explanation: Performing repeated physical movements that don't serve a clear practical purpose.

Examples: Flapping hands when excited or upset, twirling or turning objects close to eyes, head banging or head nodding.

16. [Name] used or played with objects in an atypical and repetitive manner.

Explanation: Interacting with objects in ways that differ from their intended use or in repetitive patterns.

Examples: Lining up toys instead of playing with them functionally, spinning wheels of toy cars repeatedly rather than driving them, focusing on parts of toys (e.g., opening/closing doors) rather than whole toy.

17. [Name] showed distress or anxiety when there was a change in their routines, environment, or planned events.

Explanation: Becoming upset or anxious when there are changes to familiar physical spaces or when objects are not in their expected places or positions.

Examples: Showing distress when furniture is moved in the room, becoming agitated when items are not in their usual places, showing distress over changes in food presentation (e.g., different plate), becoming upset when lights are dimmed or brightened.

18. [Name] insisted on performing specific rituals or actions in a particular manner or order.

Explanation: Following precise, self-imposed routines or sequences that must be completed in an exact way.

Examples: Needing to touch certain objects in a specific order, following the same path when moving through spaces, having a fixed order for getting dressed.

19. [Name] showed a restricted or atypical interest in specific topics, activities, or sensory stimuli.

Explanation: Showing intense focus on particular subjects or activities beyond what would be typical.

Examples: Repeatedly watching the same video segments, collecting specific items extensively, repeatedly seeking certain sensory experiences.

20. [Name] showed unusual reactions to sensory stimuli.

Explanation: Responding to sensory experiences (sounds, textures, lights, movements) in ways that differ from typical responses.

Examples: Covering ears in response to everyday sounds, seeking deep pressure or tight spaces, showing unusual fascination with certain textures.

Social Interaction Items Scales

Frequency: How often did this behavior occur today?
  • Not at all
  • Once or twice
  • Three times or more
Autonomy of the behavior: How autonomous was this behavior?

How much did the child independently initiate and carry out a social interaction or behavior? The item considers whether your child started the behavior on their own, required some help to get started, or relied on others to take the lead. It also looks at whether your child was able to continue the interaction independently or needed ongoing support.

  • Other-initiated and led: Behavior mostly guided by someone else.
  • Jointly initiated and/or somewhat independent: More collaborative and mutually initiated, child may take some initiative but still needs support.
  • Child-initiated and independent: Child spontaneously starts and maintains interaction with minimal guidance.
Appropriateness of the behavior: How appropriate was this behavior?

How well the child's behavior fit the situation or social expectations. It considers whether the behavior was suitable for the setting, age-appropriate, and socially acceptable.

  • Not appropriate: Behavior clearly not suited to the situation or social expectations.
  • Somewhat appropriate: Behavior showed effort but was only partly fitting.
  • Appropriate: Behavior was well-suited to the situation, age-appropriate, and socially fitting.
Scale for Item 29: [1] None of the possible times to [5] All of the possible times.
Social Interaction

The following items focus on different types of social behaviors your child may have displayed today. You’ll first rate how often each behavior occurred. If the behavior occurred more than once, additional questions will appear asking how independently your child carried out the behavior and how appropriate it was for the situation.

21. [Name] initiated a social interaction.

Explanation: Starting contact with another person (an adult or a child) by themselves through verbal or nonverbal means.

Examples: Approaching a parent to show them something, calling someone's name to get their attention, tapping another child's shoulder to play, using gestures to invite someone to join in an activity.

22. [Name] participated in back-and-forth social interaction.

Explanation: Taking turns in a conversation or activity with another person.

Examples: Answering when spoken to and then asking a question back, throwing and catching a ball with someone else, playing simple turn-taking games.

23. [Name] intentionally shared how they were feeling with others.

Explanation: Deliberately telling or showing others their emotions or feelings.

Examples: Saying "I'm tired" or "I'm hungry", Saying "This is boring" or "I don't like this game", Yelling "That's not fair!" during play, Showing an adult their scraped knee and looking sad, Crossing arms, frowning, Crying while looking at an adult for help, Hiding behind someone to show fear or discomfort, Running to a caregiver and saying "I love you!" or giving a spontaneous hug.

24. [Name] responded to the emotions of others.

Explanation: Noticing and reacting to how someone else is feeling.

Examples: Trying to help when someone is crying, laughing when others laugh, asking what's wrong when someone looks upset, moving away when someone seems angry, showing a concerned facial expression when seeing someone else in distress.

25. [Name] shared their own interests or experiences with another person.

Explanation: Talking about or showing something they liked or something that happened to them.

Examples: Showing off a drawing they made, telling about something that happened at school, bringing over a favorite toy to show someone, pointing out something interesting they noticed.

26. [Name] appeared to enjoy interacting with others.

Explanation: Seeming happy or pleased when spending time with other people.

Examples: Smiling during conversations, eagerly looking for others to play with, getting excited when a familiar person arrives, spending more time in group activities than alone.

27. [Name] imitated actions or behaviors.

Explanation: Copying what other people do or say.

Examples: Making the same hand movements as someone else, repeating phrases they hear, trying to use utensils after watching others eat, copying dance moves.

28. [Name] displayed affection towards others.

Explanation: Doing or saying things that show they care about or like someone.

Examples: Giving hugs or kisses, saying "I love you," sharing a favorite toy with someone, wanting to be close to people they like, holding someone's hand.

29. [Name] paid attention to things another person showed them today.

Explanation: Looking at or focusing on what someone else points out to them.

Examples: Looking at a book when someone points to it, following someone's pointing finger with their eyes, checking out an object when someone holds it up, turning to look when someone says "look at that".

Communication

30. Today [Name] used ___ individual gestures-signs/sounds/words/AAC symbols.

Explanation: This is the total number of different communication units used throughout the day. It can include gestures (like pointing or waving), signs (e.g., sign language), vocalizations or sounds (like “uh-oh” or “mmm”), spoken words, or AAC symbols (from a device or communication board).

31. On average today [Name] put together ___ gestures-signs/sounds/words/AAC symbols to communicate one message.

Explanation: This refers to how many communication units were typically combined when trying to get a message across. For example, saying or signing “more juice” uses two units.

32. On average today [Name] had about ___ turns in conversations.

Explanation: This refers to how many times your child took a turn in a back-and-forth interaction with someone. A turn includes responding to someone or initiating something new (like answering a question or asking one).

Examples: Your child pointed to the TV (1st turn), you asked, “Do you want to watch something?” (2nd turn), and your child nodded or said “yes” (3rd turn). That’s three turns in one short conversation.

33. [Name] sought or directed others attention.

Explanation: Your child tried to get someone to look at something or pay attention to them.

Examples: Pointing to a plane in the sky and saying “Look!”, Tapping your arm and showing a toy, Calling someone’s name to get their attention, Looking at you and then at something they want you to notice.

34. [Name] showed he/she wants an object.

Explanation: Your child made it clear they wanted something, using gestures, sounds, words, or signs.

Examples: Reaching for a snack, Saying or signing “ball” while looking at it, Pulling your hand toward a shelf, Pointing at a toy on the table.

35. [Name] responded spontaneously to other people.

Explanation: Your child replied to someone without being asked or prompted to do so.

Examples: Answering a question without being told to, Laughing or smiling back when someone smiled at them, Saying “hi” when someone walked in the room, Responding when someone talks to them, even if it wasn’t a direct question.

36a. [Name] followed familiar one-step directions.

Explanation: Your child followed a simple direction they already know, where only one thing needs to be done.

Examples: “Sit down” → and your child sits, “Get your shoes” → and they go get them.

36b. [Name] followed familiar multi-step directions.

Explanation: Your child followed a direction they already know or hear often, where they needed to do two or more things in order.

Examples: "Pick up your backpack and put it by the door" → and your child grabs their backpack and places it next to the door.

37a. [Name] followed unfamiliar one-step directions.

Explanation: Your child followed a simple direction that was new or that they don’t usually hear, where only one thing needed to be done.

Examples: "Put the book on the second shelf" → and your child places the book, "Press the green button" → and your child presses the correct button.

37b. [Name] followed unfamiliar multi-step directions.

Explanation: Your child followed a new or less familiar direction, where they needed to do two or more things in order.

Examples: "Take off your shoes, put them in the closet, and wash your hands" → your child removes their shoes, walks to the closet, and places the shoes inside, then goes to the sink to wash their hands.

Play

38. In how much of [Name]’s play he/she...

a. Preferred playing alone, showing minimal interest in playing with others

Explanation: Your child mostly chose to play alone and didn’t seem very interested in what other kids were doing.

Examples: Playing with toys by themselves while ignoring other children nearby.

b. Watched others play without joining in

Explanation: Your child watched other kids playing but didn’t try to join or play with them.

Examples: Sitting and watching kids play soccer without trying to join the game.

c. Shared the same space or materials with others but played separately.

Explanation: Your child played close to other children, often using the same toys or materials, but stayed focused on their own play. They did not work together, or interact much, even though they were side-by-side.

Examples: Two children playing with the same set of building blocks, each making their own tower without helping each other. Children are painting side-by-side at the same table, but each works on their own separate picture. Children in the sandbox, each digging on their own without playing together.

d. Interacted with others during play (e.g., sharing or chatting) while still doing different activities.

Explanation: Your child played near other children, shared toys or materials, and talked with them during play. They noticed and responded to each other, but each child still worked on their own separate activity without a shared goal.

Examples: Two children sitting at a table with LEGO blocks, talking about what they are building, but each making a different structure. Children painting side-by-side, sharing paints and chatting, but each creating a different picture. Kids at a play area asking each other for turns with toys, but still playing their own separate games.

e. Played with others, actively collaborating

Explanation: Your child played with other kids in a way where they worked together, shared ideas, or had a common goal.

Examples: Building a LEGO castle together or pretending to run a restaurant in a game of make-believe.

39. How much of [Name]'s play involved...

a. Exploring toys or objects by touching, banging, shaking, or moving them

Explanation: Your child explored toys or objects mainly by touching, banging, shaking, or moving them around to learn about them, without a clear goal.

Examples: Shaking a rattle to hear the sound. Banging blocks together. Running fingers over the surface of a toy to feel its texture.

b. Using two or more items to interact together (or took them apart)

Explanation: Your child played by connecting, combining, or separating two or more objects, focusing on how they go together or come apart.

Examples: Stacking cups or blocks. Fitting puzzle pieces together. Taking apart a toy car or a building made of blocks.

c. Using toys in ways they are intended to be used

Explanation: Your child used toys or objects for their expected purpose, the way they are usually meant to be used.

Examples: Rolling a toy car across the floor. Pushing buttons on a toy phone to "make a call." Putting food into a toy shopping cart.

d. Using objects to represent something else during play or engaged in pretend play scenarios

Explanation: Your child used an object to stand for something else or acted out simple pretend situations.

Examples: Pretending a stick is a magic wand. Using a banana as a phone. Making animal noises while moving toy animals around.

e. Creating and acting out imaginary scenarios or stories

Explanation: Your child invented pretend worlds, characters, or stories and acted them out through play.

Examples: Pretending to be a superhero saving the world. Setting up a pretend restaurant and taking "orders." Creating a story where dolls go on an adventure.
